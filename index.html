How It All Works

🔄
Aquaponics is a closed-loop system — fish waste feeds the plants, plants clean the water, and clean water goes back to the fish. Nothing is wasted. The solar panels power the pumps that keep everything moving. Once it's running, the system is largely self-sustaining with minimal inputs beyond fish feed.
🔄 The Nutrient Cycle — Step by Step
1
Fish Eat & Produce Waste
Your catfish and perch eat pellet feed twice a day. They digest what they need and excrete the rest as ammonia directly into the water. Fish also release ammonia through their gills as they breathe. This ammonia is toxic to fish at high concentrations — but it's also the raw fuel the whole system runs on.
2
Bacteria Convert the Ammonia
Beneficial bacteria — mainly Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter — colonize every wet surface in your system: the pool walls, the net cup media, the pipe walls, the raft board roots. These bacteria convert ammonia first into nitrite (still toxic) and then into nitrate (safe and usable). This two-step process is called the nitrogen cycle and is why you cycle your system for 4–6 weeks before adding fish.
3
Plants Absorb the Nitrates
Nitrates are essentially liquid fertilizer. Your lettuce, herbs, kale, strawberries, and every other plant in the towers and raft boards absorb nitrates through their roots as their primary nitrogen source. The more fish you have, the more nutrients are available, and the faster your plants grow. A well-balanced system grows plants 2–4× faster than soil gardening because the nutrients are delivered directly to the roots 24/7.
4
Clean Water Returns to Fish
As the plants take up nitrates, dissolved oxygen from the airstones replenishes the water, and filtered water cycles back to the fish tank. The raft board plant roots also act as a biological filter — they trap fine particles and absorb nutrients directly from the water column. The fish get clean, oxygenated water and the cycle begins again.
🔧 What Each Component Does
Component Role in the System
Pool (fish tank) The heart of the system. Houses your 125–175 fish and holds 2,893 gallons of water. The large water volume acts as a buffer — it takes longer to go wrong, which gives you time to catch and fix problems before fish are lost. More water = more stable chemistry.
Catfish Bottom-dwelling primary food fish. High waste producers — their ammonia output is the main nutrient source for your plants. They're forgiving of water quality fluctuations, which makes them ideal for a first system. Harvest at 12–18 months.
Yellow Perch Mid-to-upper water column fish. Complement the catfish without competing for space or food. More cold-tolerant than catfish, so they keep the system productive deeper into fall. Slower growing but excellent eating. Harvest at 18–24 months.
Airstones + Air Pump The life-support system. Continuously dissolve oxygen into the water so fish can breathe and aerobic bacteria can thrive. Without adequate dissolved oxygen, fish suffocate and the beneficial bacterial colony collapses. This is why the air pump is wired to run 24/7 with no timer — it never turns off.
Floating Raft Boards Foam boards floating directly on the pool surface with net cups holding plants. Plant roots hang directly into the fish water with no pumping required — the simplest part of the system. Roots naturally filter the water and absorb nutrients. Best for leafy greens, herbs, and shallow-rooted plants.
PVC Grow Towers Vertical growing columns mounted on the deck. The tower pump pulls water from the pool and pushes it through a supply manifold to the top of each tower. Water trickles down through the grow media, wetting plant roots, and drains back into the pool. Ball valves on each tower let you shut off individual towers for maintenance without stopping the whole system.
Tower Pump + Manifold A single submersible pump in the pool pulls water up to a 1-inch PVC supply line running the length of the tower row. Half-inch branch lines with ball valves drop water to the top of each tower. The pump runs on a 15-minute-on / 45-minute-off timer to save energy while still delivering regular water and nutrients to tower roots.
Biofilter (bacteria) Invisible but essential. Beneficial bacteria colonies live on every wet surface — pool walls, pipe interiors, grow media, raft roots. They're what convert toxic fish waste into plant-usable nutrients. The 4–6 week cycling period before adding fish exists entirely to grow this bacterial colony. Without it, ammonia and nitrite spike and kill fish within days.
Solar Panels + Battery 400W of solar panels charge a LiFePO4 battery through an MPPT charge controller. The battery powers the inverter, which provides AC power to both pumps. St. George's exceptional sun means the panels charge the battery fully most days, with the battery carrying the system through nights and cloudy periods. The whole electrical system is independent of the grid.
Shade cloth In St. George summers, pool water temperature is your biggest threat. Warm water holds less dissolved oxygen, which stresses fish. A 30–40% shade cloth over part of the system reduces water temperature by 5–10°F on peak summer days, directly protecting fish health and preventing oxygen crashes during the hottest months.
💧 How the Water Moves
Tower Loop
Timer on → Submersible pump activates in the pool floor.
Water is pushed up through ½" supply lines to the manifold.
Manifold distributes water to all 10 tower tops simultaneously.
Water trickles down through grow media, wetting roots along the way.
Tower drains return water to pool via gravity.
Timer off → Roots stay moist from residual water in media.
Cycle repeats every hour: 15 min on / 45 min off.
Raft System
No pumping required — rafts float passively on the pool surface.
Plant roots hang directly into the water at all times.
Fish swim below and their waste rises through the water column.
Roots absorb nutrients and dissolved oxygen continuously.
Roots also trap fine suspended particles, acting as a biofilter.
Airstones below the rafts keep water oxygenated for both fish and roots.
The simplest and most productive part of the whole system.
🪨 Grow Bed Water Flow — Flood and Drain
Flood Phase (timer ON or siphon filling)
Dedicated grow bed pump in the pool activates.
Water pumps up through 1" supply manifold to both beds simultaneously.
Water floods from the bottom up through the grow media.
Flood rises until it reaches the standpipe top — about 1" below media surface.
Roots are saturated with nutrient-rich water.
Drain Phase (bell siphon triggers)
Bell siphon triggers automatically when water reaches standpipe height.
Entire bed drains rapidly through the bulkhead fitting in the bed bottom.
Drained water returns to pool via gravity drain line.
Roots are exposed to air — this oxygen phase is critical for root health.
Pump can stay on — siphon breaks at low water, bed refloods. Cycle repeats every 30–45 min.
⚙️
Beds run on their own pump circuit. Keep the grow bed pump separate from the tower pump so you can adjust flood cycles for each zone independently. Towers run on a 15-min-on/45-min-off timer. Grow beds run on bell siphons that cycle automatically based on flood depth — no timer needed once the standpipe height is set correctly. Both pumps draw from the same pool water.
⚖️ Keeping the System in Balance
📊
The golden ratio: A healthy aquaponic system needs roughly 1 square foot of plant growing space for every 1 gallon of fish tank volume, or about 1 lb of fish for every 5–10 gallons of water. Your system at target stocking (125–175 fish, 140+ plant sites, 2,893 gallons) is well within this range. When balanced, your water test results should stay stable week over week without intervention.
Too Many Fish
Ammonia and nitrite spike. Plants can't absorb nutrients fast enough. Water turns cloudy or smells bad. Fish become lethargic and may gasp at the surface. Fix: reduce fish load, do a partial water change, increase aeration.
In Balance ✓
Ammonia near zero. Nitrite near zero. Nitrates between 20–80 ppm. Fish active and feeding. Plants growing vigorously. Water clear. This is what you're aiming for — weekly water tests confirm everything is stable.
Too Many Plants
Nitrates drop too low, plants yellow and growth slows. Fish are fine. Fix: add more fish gradually, or supplement with a small amount of aquaponic-safe liquid nutrients. This problem is less common and less urgent than the reverse.
🧪 Water Testing — Your Most Important Habit
⚠️
Use the API Freshwater Master Test Kit (liquid drops, not strips). Test strips are not accurate enough for aquaponics. The liquid kit tests ammonia, nitrite, nitrate, and pH. Test every 2–3 days during cycling, weekly once the system is stable, and immediately any time fish behavior seems off.
Parameter Target Range What It Tells You If It's Wrong
Ammonia (NH₃) 0–0.5 ppm Fresh fish waste. Rising ammonia = system is producing more than bacteria can process. Above 1 ppm: stop feeding fish for 24–48 hrs, do a 20% water change, check aeration.
Nitrite (NO₂) 0–0.5 ppm Intermediate conversion product. Toxic to fish. High nitrite = bacteria colony not keeping up. Above 1 ppm: reduce feeding, partial water change, add more bacterial media surface area.
Nitrate (NO₃) 20–80 ppm End product — plant fertilizer. Should always be present. Too high means plants aren't keeping up. Above 150 ppm: do a partial water change, add more plant sites, or reduce fish feed temporarily.
pH 6.8–7.2 Affects how well bacteria and plants can function. Drifts down over time naturally. Below 6.5: add small amounts of potassium carbonate or calcium carbonate to raise. Above 7.8: add pH down solution slowly.
Temperature 70–76°F Affects fish metabolism, bacterial activity, and dissolved oxygen levels simultaneously. Above 82°F in summer: deploy shade cloth, increase aeration. Below 60°F in winter: catfish stop eating and growing.
Dissolved Oxygen >6 ppm Critical for fish and bacteria. Drops in warm water and at high fish density. Fish gasping at surface = emergency. Add more aeration immediately, reduce fish density if needed.

📅
How and when to stock: Stock both species on the same day, after your system has fully cycled (ammonia and nitrite both reading zero). Do not introduce catfish first and perch later — stocking together prevents either species from establishing territorial dominance. Start with 50–60 fish total (roughly 30–35 of each), watch water parameters for 2 weeks, then add the remainder if ammonia and nitrite stay near zero.
🎣
Harvest sequence: Catfish will reach harvest weight first at 12–18 months. Harvest them in batches of 20–30 at a time rather than all at once — this keeps the bioload stable and your plants fed. Immediately restock with new catfish fingerlings when you harvest. Perch harvest follows 6–12 months later. Combined potential yield from one full grow-out cycle: 100–225 lbs of food fish.
💧
Target water parameters for mixed catfish + perch system:

Temperature: 70–76°F — the overlap zone where both species feed actively. Below 65°F catfish slow down; below 50°F they stop eating. Perch tolerate cooler water better.
pH: 6.8–7.2 — both species and your plants are comfortable here.
Ammonia: below 1 ppm — above this, fish stress and plant growth suffers.
Nitrite: below 1 ppm — toxic to fish at elevated levels.
Nitrate: below 80 ppm — high nitrates fuel plant growth but become toxic to fish at very high levels. Water changes help if nitrates climb.
Dissolved oxygen: above 6 ppm — size your aeration for perch's higher oxygen demand, not just catfish tolerance. This is the most important parameter to monitor in St. George summers when warm water holds less oxygen naturally.

🍅 Fruiting Plants (advanced — mature system only)
Each entry below includes growing requirements, raft setup details, support/training notes, and harvest timing specific to floating raft culture. Only attempt fruiting plants after your system has been running at least 6 months with a full fish load.
Cherry tomatoes ☀️ Summer ⚠️ Mature System 🫙 WB 🥫 PC
Most practical tomato for raft culture. Determinate compact varieties keep root mass manageable and reduce top-heaviness. High nutrient demand — only viable in a mature system with 100+ fish at full load.
Net cup setup: Use a 3" net cup minimum — 4" is better. Fill with hydroton. Transplant a 6–8 week old seedling that has been hardened off. The root ball should fill the cup snugly.
Staking: Push a bamboo stake or 1/4" fiberglass rod straight down through the net cup into the water before planting. Stake must reach the raft board and extend 4–5 ft above it. Tie stem to stake loosely with soft plant ties every 8–10 inches as it grows. Without a firm stake the plant will topple and snap at the stem.
Raft board spacing: One tomato plant per 12" × 12" section minimum — they need room. Limit to 3–4 plants per raft board. Do not crowd with other large plants.
Pollination: No bees reach pool-side plants easily. Hand-pollinate daily during flowering — tap flower clusters firmly or use a small electric toothbrush held against the flower stem for 2–3 seconds. Do this in the morning when flowers are open. Missing pollination = no fruit.
Pruning: Pinch suckers (growth emerging from the crotch between stem and branch) weekly. Unpruned suckers become full branches and the plant becomes unmanageable on a raft. Determinate varieties need less pruning than indeterminate.
Varieties that work best: Tumbling Tom, Patio, Bush Early Girl, Tiny Tim, Red Robin. Avoid: Big Boy, Beefsteak, any indeterminate heirloom over 4 ft.
Harvest timing: 60–80 days from transplant. Pick when fully colored but slightly firm — fully soft tomatoes fall off and sink. Check daily at peak season.
Canning: {WB} Add 1 tbsp bottled lemon juice per pint, process 35 min. {PC} Plain at 11 lbs PSI, 25 min pints. Best canning crop in the system — grow as many as your raft space allows.
Dwarf / compact peppers ☀️ Summer ⚠️ Mature System 🫙 WB 🥫 PC
The most raft-friendly fruiting vegetable after cherry tomatoes. Compact root system, upright growth, and St. George heat suits them perfectly. Prolific producers from June through September.
Net cup setup: 3" net cup with hydroton. Peppers have a more compact root system than tomatoes — they establish well in standard net cups without crowding the raft. Transplant at 4–6 weeks old, after last frost risk.
Staking: Peppers are stockier than tomatoes but still need support once fruit loads develop. A single 2–3 ft bamboo stake per plant tied loosely at two points is sufficient. The stem is thicker and less likely to snap than tomatoes, but heavy fruit sets can pull branches sideways — add a second tie if needed.
Raft board spacing: One plant per 8" × 8" section. Compact varieties stay tidy. Can fit 4–6 pepper plants per raft board if not mixing with tomatoes.
Pollination: Same as tomatoes — hand-pollinate by tapping flowers or using an electric toothbrush. Peppers are easier to pollinate than tomatoes; gentle tapping is usually enough.
Variety selection matters: Dwarf or compact labelled varieties are essential. Good choices: Lunchbox mini sweets (red, orange, yellow), Pot-A-Peno jalapeño, NuMex Twilight ornamental chili, Cayennetta. Avoid: California Wonder bell pepper, Anaheim, any variety labelled "large" or growing over 24".
Harvest timing: 70–90 days from transplant. Harvest green for earlier production or leave to ripen to red/yellow/orange for sweeter flavor. Frequent harvesting encourages more fruit set — do not let peppers fully dry on the plant.
Canning: {WB} Pickled peppers in vinegar brine, 10 min pints. {PC} Plain roasted and peeled at 11 lbs PSI, 35 min half-pints.
Cucumbers (bush / compact) ☀️ Summer ⚠️ Mature System 🫙 WB
The trickiest fruiting vegetable for rafts — vining habit requires active management. Richly rewarding when done right. Only attempt with at least 150 fish in the pool and nitrates consistently above 40 ppm.
Net cup setup: 4" net cup required — cucumber roots are aggressive and will fill a 3" cup within weeks. Pack with hydroton, transplant at 3–4 weeks old. Cucumbers are faster-growing than tomatoes and establish quickly on rafts.
Trellis — critical: Cucumbers cannot be staked like tomatoes. They need a trellis they can climb. Options: (1) Run a horizontal wire or string line at 5–6 ft above the raft board using pool frame posts as anchors, then run vertical strings down to each plant — this is a "string trellis" and the cleanest method. (2) A freestanding A-frame trellis placed straddling the raft board. Without a trellis, vines trail into the pool, root in the water, and become unmanageable within 2 weeks.
Training: Guide the main vine upward onto the trellis daily when young — it grows fast. Once the main vine reaches the top of the trellis, pinch the growing tip to redirect energy to lateral branches and fruit. Remove any vine tips trying to grow downward into the pool.
Raft board spacing: One cucumber plant per raft board — maximum two if the board is large. They consume significant lateral space and nutrient load.
Pollination: Cucumbers have separate male and female flowers on the same plant. Female flowers have a tiny proto-cucumber at the base. Transfer pollen from a male flower to a female using a small paintbrush or cotton swab, or tap the plants vigorously to release pollen. Do this in the morning.
Variety selection: Bush Pickle, Spacemaster, Patio Snacker, Bush Champion. All stay compact with shorter vines. Avoid slicing varieties like Straight Eight which grow extremely long vines.
Harvest timing: 50–60 days from transplant. Pick daily at peak — cucumbers go from perfect to overgrown and seedy in 48 hours. For pickling, harvest at 3–4 inches (gherkin size) for best texture and crunch.
Canning: {WB} Dill pickles — vinegar-dill-garlic brine, 10 min pints / 15 min quarts. The small bush pickle varieties are ideal — pick small and firm. Never can cucumbers that have softened.
Tomatillos ☀️ Summer ⚠️ Mature System 🫙 WB
Easier than tomatoes on rafts — more heat tolerant, naturally compact, and the papery husk protects fruit from humidity issues at pool-side. Must grow two plants for cross-pollination.
Net cup setup: 3" net cup with hydroton. Tomatillos are vigorous growers — transplant at 4–6 weeks old after hardening off. They establish quickly and handle raft conditions well.
Staking: Tomatillos grow 2–4 ft tall and spread outward. A single 4 ft stake plus one or two horizontal ties keeps them upright. They are stockier and less prone to toppling than tomatoes but the fruit weight on outer branches can pull them down — clip stray branches to the main stake.
Two-plant rule: Unlike tomatoes and peppers, tomatillos require cross-pollination between two separate plants. One plant will flower heavily but produce almost no fruit. Plant two minimum and space them 12" apart on the same raft board or on adjacent boards. Tap flowers to transfer pollen between the two plants.
Raft spacing: Two plants per raft board is the practical limit alongside other plants. They spread more than peppers and less than cucumbers.
Harvest timing: 75–100 days from transplant. Ready when the papery husk fills out and turns tan/brown and the fruit inside feels firm. Do not harvest early — tomatillos taste sharp and astringent before fully ripe. Ripe fruit sometimes bursts through the husk.
Canning: {WB} Naturally high acid — water bath safe without adding lemon juice, though 1 tbsp per pint improves safety margin. Roast first for salsa verde, process 15 min pints. Freeze the excess roasted puree for year-round salsa verde.
Green beans / snap beans ☀️ Summer ⚠️ Mature System 🫙 WB 🥫 PC
One of the more forgiving fruiting crops on rafts — bush varieties stay compact, roots do not get enormous, and they produce heavily. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria on roots actually benefit the system.
Net cup setup: 3" net cup with hydroton. Sow seeds directly into the net cup — no transplanting needed. Push 2–3 seeds into the hydroton, 1" deep. Thin to the strongest seedling once sprouted. Direct-sowing works better than transplanting for beans as they do not like root disturbance.
No staking required: Bush bean varieties stay 15–18" tall and support themselves. This is one of the big advantages over other fruiting crops — no staking, no trellis, minimal management. They are genuinely low-maintenance once established.
Raft board spacing: One plant per 6" × 6" section — you can fit more beans per raft board than any other fruiting crop. 6–8 plants per board is reasonable. Stagger plantings for continuous harvest.
Pollination: Beans are self-pollinating — no hand pollination needed. The flower pollinates itself before it even opens fully. This makes them one of the most reliable fruiting crops in an enclosed or low-bee environment.
Variety selection: Blue Lake Bush, Provider, Contender, Roma II, Bush Kentucky Wonder. All stay compact and produce heavily. Avoid pole beans entirely — they grow 6–8 ft and require substantial trellis infrastructure.
Harvest timing: 50–60 days from transplant or 55–65 days from seed. Pick when pods are firm, bright green, and snap cleanly — before seeds bulge visibly inside. Check every 2 days at peak. Leaving pods too long tells the plant to stop producing. Remove any yellow or bulging pods immediately.
Succession planting: Beans produce for 3–4 weeks then decline. Start new net cups every 3 weeks to maintain continuous harvest through the summer.
Canning: {WB} Dilly beans — pack vertically in pint jars with dill, garlic, pepper flakes and vinegar brine, 10 min. {PC} Plain green beans at 11 lbs PSI, 20 min pints — best canned young and tender.
Zucchini / summer squash ☀️ Summer ⚠️ Mature System 🫙 WB 🥫 PC
The most productive plant you can put on a raft, and also the one that most requires restraint — one or two plants will produce more than a household can eat. Root system is large; only attempt in a mature high-fish system.
Net cup setup: 4" net cup required — zucchini roots are massive. Better yet, use a 5-gallon net pot if your raft board has room. Sow seeds directly into the cup (2 seeds, thin to 1) or transplant at 2–3 weeks old. Do not over-transplant — zucchini does not like being moved after 3 weeks.
Vining management: Most "bush" zucchini varieties still produce trailing secondary vines. Train these over the pool edge and onto the deck or ground rather than letting them curl back over the raft. A single piece of rebar or a pool noodle zip-tied to the raft edge gives the vine a ramp to climb over. If a vine tip reaches soil it will try to root — cut it back.
Spacing: One zucchini plant per raft board — it will eventually take over that board. Do not plant zucchini on the same board as tomatoes or cucumbers. Reserve one board for zucchini only.
Pollination: Zucchini produces large separate male and female flowers. Female flowers have a tiny immature squash at the base. Male flowers appear first — do not worry if early flowers fall off without fruiting. Once females appear, use a paintbrush to transfer pollen from a male to female flower in the morning when both are open. One male can pollinate multiple females. Poor pollination produces misshapen or rotting fruit.
Leaf management: Zucchini leaves are enormous and will shade neighboring raft plants. Remove any leaf that is yellowing or lying flat on the raft board surface — they trap moisture and encourage rot at the base. Keep the crown of the plant open and airy.
Variety selection: Patio Star, Bush Baby, Astia, Eight Ball (round variety — compact and productive). Avoid: Black Beauty, Grey Zucchini, any variety without "bush" or "compact" in the name.
Harvest timing: 50–60 days from transplant. Harvest when 6–8 inches long — do not let zucchini grow to baseball bat size. Large zucchini taste bland, have tough seeds, and signal the plant to slow down production. Check daily at peak. A zucchini can double in size overnight in warm weather.
Canning: {WB} Zucchini relish or bread-and-butter zucchini pickles, 10 min pints — best use of surplus. {PC} Plain diced zucchini at 11 lbs PSI, 30 min pints — texture softens, best in soups and stews after canning.
🪨 Media Grow Beds — 2 beds × 25–35 sites each (~50–70 total plant sites)

The two grow beds sit on the south side of the pool, opposite the curved tower deck. Each is an IBC tote half (cut horizontally from a 250-gallon tote) filled with expanded clay or washed pea gravel. A separate pump or a tee off the tower manifold floods each bed on a timer or bell siphon cycle. These beds handle everything that needs deep root support — fruiting vegetables the raft boards cannot support at all.

Setup: Each bed needs a bulkhead fitting in the bottom for drain return to the pool, a standpipe for flood level control, and either a mechanical timer or bell siphon for automated drain. Flood to 1" below media surface, drain fully, repeat every 30–45 minutes. Grow beds run on their own pump circuit or via a tee off the main tower pump — see the updated materials list and plumbing notes below.

Weekend 2
Deck Frame + Pool + Grow Bed Positioning
Set posts into post bases, attach beams, hang joists with joist hangers. Lay decking boards. Install tower mounting rail along pool-facing edge. Set up pool — assemble frame, install liner, begin filling. Set up airstones and air pump. Position the two IBC grow beds on the south side of the pool — confirm drain lines can reach back to the pool and pump lines are not excessively long. Cut the IBC tote in half if you have not done so already.
Weekend 3
Towers + Grow Beds + Plumbing
Cut tower holes in PVC at staggered positions. Cap bottoms, mount towers to deck rail. Assemble tower manifold and run branch lines with ball valves. Install tower pump and test flow. Separately: install bulkhead fittings in grow bed bottoms, set up bell siphons with standpipes, run supply manifold from grow bed pump to both beds with ball valves. Fill grow beds with rinsed media. Test flood-and-drain cycle on each bed — standpipe height controls flood depth; bell siphon should trigger reliably and drain completely. Adjust as needed.
Week 4–8
System Cycling (No Fish Yet)
Dose with pure ammonia or small daily fish food additions to feed bacteria growth. Test water every 2–3 days. System is cycled when ammonia and nitrite both read 0 and nitrates are climbing. Float raft boards and start leafy greens during this period — they tolerate the chemistry swings.
Week 6–8
Stock Fish (Conservatively)
Start with 50–60 fish total combined — roughly 30–35 catfish and 20–25 perch. Stock both species on the same day. Stocking together prevents territory dominance. Watch water parameters every 2–3 days. If ammonia and nitrite both stay below 0.5 ppm after 2 weeks, add the remaining fish to reach your target of 125–175 combined. Never add large batches at once — stress and ammonia spikes kill fish fast.
Month 3+
Full Production Mode
System balanced, fish growing, plants producing. Begin staggered planting in towers — replace harvested plants immediately to maintain continuous production. Monitor water parameters weekly. Partial water changes (10–15%) every 2–3 weeks help dilute accumulating minerals.
Month 12–18
First Catfish Harvest
Catfish reach 1–1.5 lbs — harvest in batches of 20–30 at a time, not all at once. Removing too many fish at once crashes the bioload and starves your plants. Order new catfish fingerlings to arrive around the same time you start harvesting so the system stays stocked continuously. Perch harvest follows 6–12 months after the catfish — they are slower growing but still a valuable food fish. Combined yield from the first full grow-out cycle: roughly 100–225 lbs of food fish total from both species.

St. George Seasonal Notes

☀️ Summer (June–Aug)
Biggest challenge. Water temps can climb dangerously. Deploy shade cloth, consider a small shade structure. Top off water lost to evaporation frequently. Monitor fish for heat stress — lethargic fish at the surface is a warning sign. Prioritize heat-tolerant plants: basil, kale, chard.
🍂 Fall / Spring
Best seasons for your system. Mild temps mean both fish species are actively feeding and growing. Excellent for cool-season crops: spinach, lettuce, arugula, cilantro. Best time to stock new fingerlings and cycle a new batch of fish.
❄️ Winter (Dec–Feb)
Catfish slow significantly below 50°F. Perch stay more active. Consider harvesting catfish before first hard freeze. A simple polycarbonate hoop cover over the system extends your growing season significantly and protects plants from frost nights.

Home Canning — Preserving Your Harvest

⚠️
Safety first: Always follow tested, USDA-approved canning recipes. Never alter ingredient ratios — acid levels and processing times are calculated for food safety. When in doubt, pressure can. Reference the National Center for Home Food Preservation at nchfp.uga.edu for complete instructions. St. George sits at ~2,880 ft elevation — add 1 lb PSI to all pressure canning recipes (standard 10 lb becomes 11 lbs).
🫙 Water Bath Canner
For high-acid foods only (pH below 4.6). Boiling water at 212°F is sufficient to destroy pathogens in acidic environments.

💡 Canning Tips for Aquaponic Produce
Harvest Timing
Pick at peak ripeness — not overripe. Tomatoes: fully red but firm. Cucumbers: small and firm. Peppers: fully colored. Overripe produce turns mushy during processing and loses flavor.
Wash Thoroughly
Aquaponic produce is clean but rinse all vegetables well before canning. Fish water is nutrient-rich and any residue on produce affects flavor. A quick soak in cool water is enough.

Harvesting Fish & Freezing for Storage

🐟
Harvest in batches, not all at once. Removing too many fish at once crashes your bioload, starves your plants, and destabilizes water chemistry. Harvest 10–20% of your fish at a time — for the pool system that means 15–30 fish per harvest session. Stagger harvests 4–6 weeks apart and your system stays productive throughout.
📏 Step 1 — Know When They Are Ready
Size Check
Catfish: Ready at 1–1.5 lbs. Easiest check — hold one up. It should feel substantial, roughly the size of a large hand. Body should be thick and rounded, not thin and flat.

Yellow Perch: Ready at 0.5–1 lb. They are naturally smaller fish. Harvest when they reach about 8–10 inches long — a satisfying pan-size fish.

A simple kitchen scale at poolside is the most reliable tool. Weigh a few fish before committing to a full harvest session.
Harvest Timing
Best time of day: early morning when water is coolest and fish are calmest.

Best season: fall or spring — mild air temps make the process more comfortable and fish stress less in cooler water.

Stop feeding fish 24–48 hours before harvesting. Empty gut means cleaner processing and better flavor.

Avoid harvesting during peak summer heat — stressed fish in warm water quality drop fast.
🪣 Step 2 — Netting Your Fish

You need a large mesh landing net (18–24 inch hoop) and a holding container — a 30–50 gallon trash can or cooler filled with pool water works well. Have your processing station set up and ready before you start netting.

WHAT YOU NEED
  • 18–24" landing net (pool net works)
  • 30–50 gal holding container with pool water
  • Small battery-powered aerator for holding container
  • Kitchen scale (to confirm harvest size)
  • Ice-filled cooler ready at processing station
  • Sharp fillet knife (8–9 inch)
  • Cutting board (dedicated, non-porous)
  • Zip-lock freezer bags or vacuum sealer bags
  • Sharpie marker for labeling bags
NETTING TECHNIQUE
  • Work slowly and quietly — sudden movement scatters fish
  • Catfish tend to school near the bottom — angle net downward
  • Perch school mid-water — net horizontally
  • Net toward a pool wall to corner fish
  • Transfer netted fish quickly to holding container
  • Use the aerator in the holding container for any fish waiting more than 10 minutes
  • Target your largest fish first — let smaller ones grow on
⚡ Step 3 — Dispatching Humanely and Quickly
💡
Dispatch immediately before processing. The fastest and most humane method for both catfish and perch is a sharp blow to the top of the head (the iki jime method or a firm knock with a heavy mallet). Death within seconds means the fish does not suffer and also means better-tasting meat — stress hormones released during a slow death degrade flesh quality.
Best Method — Brain Spike (Iki Jime)
A sharp metal spike (ice pick, screwdriver, or dedicated iki jime tool) inserted directly into the brain — just behind the eyes on the top of the head. Instant death, zero stress, best meat quality. Takes about 2 seconds per fish once you learn the spot. Recommended for any fish you plan to freeze or cook fresh.
Simpler Method — Sharp Blow
A firm, decisive strike to the top of the head with a heavy blunt object — a wooden mallet, thick dowel, or the spine of a large knife. Hold fish firmly, strike once with confidence. Works well for catfish given their thick skulls. Followed immediately by bleeding (cutting gills) for best quality.
🩸 Step 4 — Bleeding (Important for Flavor)
Immediately after dispatching, cut through the gills or make a shallow cut behind each gill plate. Hold the fish headdown over a bucket for 30–60 seconds. Blood draining from the flesh makes a significant difference in flavor — particularly for catfish, which can have a stronger taste if not bled. Place bled fish immediately into your ice-filled cooler. Keep fish on ice throughout the entire filleting process.
🔪 Step 5 — Filleting
⚠️
Catfish warning: Catfish have sharp spines on their dorsal (top) and pectoral (side) fins. These can cause painful puncture wounds. Hold catfish firmly behind the pectoral spines — wrap your hand around the body just behind the spines with your thumb and forefinger on either side. Never squeeze the belly. Wear cut-resistant gloves if you are new to handling them.
Step Action Detail
1 Position the fish Lay fish flat on cutting board, belly facing away from you. Head to your left if right-handed. Fish should be cold — warm fish are slippery and harder to fillet cleanly.
2 First cut — behind the head Make a vertical cut just behind the pectoral fins, angled slightly toward the head. Cut down until you feel the spine — do not cut through it.
3 Run knife along spine Turn blade horizontal, keeping it flat against the spine. Using long smooth strokes, slice from head toward the tail, letting the spine guide the blade. Keep the knife as close to the spine as possible — this is where most meat is lost by beginners.
4 Free the fillet When you reach the tail, cut through the skin to free the fillet. Flip fish and repeat on the other side. You now have two fillets.
5 Remove rib bones Lay fillet skin-side down. Slide knife under the rib cage and slice the ribs away from the meat in one smooth cut. Angle blade slightly upward to avoid losing meat.
6 Remove skin (catfish) Catfish skin is edible but most people prefer it removed. Hold tail end of fillet skin-side down, insert knife between skin and meat at the tail end, then angle blade slightly down and push forward in a sawing motion while pulling skin back. Perch skin is thin and mild — can be left on or removed by preference.
7 Pin bone check Run a finger along the center line of the fillet. You will feel a row of small pin bones. Pull these out with needle-nose pliers or fish tweezers — work against the grain (pull toward the head end). Takes 30 seconds per fillet and makes a big difference in eating quality.
🧊 Step 6 — Rinsing and Prepping for Freezing

Rinse each fillet under cold running water and pat dry with paper towels. Keep everything cold — if fillets warm up during processing they get soft and lose texture. Work quickly or keep a bowl of ice water nearby to hold finished fillets while you process the rest.

Optional brine soak (recommended for catfish): Soak fillets in cold salted water (1/4 cup salt per gallon of water) for 30–60 minutes before freezing. This firms the flesh, draws out any remaining blood, and noticeably improves flavor. Rinse after soaking and pat dry before packaging.

📦 Step 7 — Packaging and Freezing
✅ Best — Vacuum Sealer
Removes all air — freezer burn is essentially eliminated. Fish stays fresh-quality for 12–18 months in the freezer. Worth the investment ($60–120) if you are doing regular harvests. FoodSaver is the most common brand. Seal individual portions (one meal per bag).
Good — Freezer Zip-Lock Bags
Use freezer-weight bags (not regular zip-locks). Press out as much air as possible before sealing — a straw to suck remaining air helps. Good for 6–9 months if well-packed. Label with species, date, and weight. Double-bag thick fillets.
Also Works — Water Pack
Place fillet in a freezer container and cover completely with cold water, then freeze. The ice block protects fish from air exposure. Good quality for 6–12 months. Works especially well for perch. Thaw in the refrigerator — dump the water and rinse before cooking.
🏷️
Always label every bag: Species (catfish or perch) · Date harvested · Weight or portion size · "Aquaponic — homegrown." When you pull a bag of your own catfish out of the freezer in February, you will want that information. Use a permanent marker directly on the bag or a freezer label.
🌡️ Thawing Correctly
Best: Overnight in the refrigerator. Slow thaw preserves texture.

Faster: Sealed bag in cold water, changing water every 30 minutes. Takes 1–2 hours.

Never: Thaw at room temperature or in warm water. Bacteria multiply rapidly above 40°F on thawing fish. Never refreeze thawed fish.

From the pool system's 125–175 fish at 1–1.5 lbs each:

At 1 lb per meal portion: 56–117 meals
At 4 meals per week: 14–29 weeks of fish

That is potentially 6+ months of protein from one grow-out cycle.

IBC System Overview

🔄
The chop-and-flip method: A standard IBC tote is cut horizontally about 35% from the top. The top section is flipped upside down and placed on top of the lower fish tank section — creating a self-contained system where the grow bed sits directly above the fish. 250–275 gallon food-grade IBC tote. Buy used from Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, or local farms. Confirm it held food-safe contents only — never use a tote that held chemicals, pesticides, or industrial fluids.

IBC Build Timeline

Day 1–2
Source and Inspect the Tote
Find a food-grade IBC tote. Confirm it held food-safe contents — never use a tote that held chemicals, pesticides, or industrial fluids. Check the label on the side of the tote cage. Rinse thoroughly multiple times before cutting. Paint the outside of the bottom section dark to prevent algae once setup is complete.
Day 3
Mark the Chop Line and Cut
Mark the cut line approximately 35% from the top of the tote — roughly 17–18 inches down from the top rim. Cut with an angle grinder or jigsaw. Wear eye protection and cut-resistant gloves. IBC plastic produces sharp edges — smooth any rough cuts with sandpaper. Flip the top section upside down to confirm it sits stably on the bottom tank before proceeding.
Day 4–5
Plumbing — Grow Bed and Tower
Drill bulk head fitting hole in grow bed bottom. Install bell siphon standpipe or timer-controlled drain. Run submersible pump supply line from tank up to grow bed inlet. Tee off supply line with ball valve for tower branch. Run 1/2" line from tee to tower top. Fill system with water and test: flood-and-drain cycle should run reliably, bell siphon should drain fully, tower should get consistent trickle. Adjust ball valve until both zones receive adequate flow.
Day 5–6
Tower Setup
Cut 6–8 plant holes in 4" PVC at staggered positions. Holes should spiral around the pipe so each plant gets light exposure. Cap bottom with small drainage holes drilled. Mount tower beside tote using pipe straps on a T-post or wooden stake. Confirm supply line feeds consistently to tower top and return drains cleanly back to tank.
Day 6
Fill Grow Bed with Media
Rinse hydroton or pea gravel thoroughly before adding — new media affects pH. Add to grow bed to about 1 inch from top. Run water through several cycles until runoff is clear. Check bell siphon triggers correctly with media weight in place — standpipe height may need adjustment.
Day 7
Aeration and Solar
Install airstones on tank bottom, connect to air pump. If using solar, mount panel south-facing at 30–35° tilt. Connect panel to charge controller, controller to battery, battery to inverter. Wire both pumps to GFCI outlet. Test all components run. Label air pump circuit — it must never be switched off.
Weeks 1–4
System Cycling — No Fish Yet
Dose system with pure ammonia every other day to feed growing bacteria colony. Test water every 2–3 days with API liquid test kit. Plant hardy greens (kale, chard, lettuce) in grow bed during cycling — they tolerate the fluctuating chemistry and their roots help establish beneficial bacteria. System is fully cycled when both ammonia AND nitrite read zero and nitrates are climbing above 20 ppm. Do not rush this step.
Week 4–5
Stock Fish — Start Conservative
Start with 12–15 fingerlings, not the full 20–30. Watch water parameters every 2 days. If ammonia and nitrite both stay below 0.5 ppm for two consecutive weeks, add remaining fish. Stock on a mild day — avoid introducing fish when air temperature exceeds 95°F. Float the bag in the tank water for 20 minutes before releasing to temperature-match.
Month 2–3
Full Production Mode
System balanced, fish growing steadily, plants producing. Fill all grow bed and tower sites. Begin succession planting — start new seedlings 3 weeks before you expect to harvest the previous crop. Test water weekly. Partial water changes of 10–15% every 2–3 weeks dilute accumulating minerals and refresh the system.
Month 12–18
First Harvest — Fish and Vegetables
Catfish reach 1–1.5 lbs. Harvest 4–8 fish at a time — never all at once. Removing too many fish at once crashes the nutrient supply to your plants. Order new fingerlings to arrive around harvest time so the system stays continuously productive. Consider pressure canning your catfish harvest for long-term storage — see the Home Canning section.
